# Business Logic Bugs: The Vulnerabilities That Hide in Plain Sight

## Introduction
When people think about web security, they often think about SQL Injection, XSS, SSRF, or broken access control. These vulnerabilities usually have recognizable patterns, payloads, and testing methodologies.

Business logic bugs are different.

They do not exist because a developer forgot to sanitize input or because a server exposes sensitive data. Instead, they appear when an application's workflows interact in ways the designers never anticipated.

In my experience, business logic bugs are among the most rewarding vulnerabilities to find because they often hide behind perfectly functioning features.

This article shares one of my findings and the mindset I use when looking for business logic issues.

## A Real Business Logic Bug I Found

<img width="1472" height="338" alt="1" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/f80c5402-6431-436d-b7f4-e1611fd04343" />

The screenshot above shows a reward for a business logic vulnerability I reported.

The application only allowed users to register with approved email domains such as `gmail.com` and `yahoo.com`. Any other domain was supposed to be rejected.

The registration flow worked as follows:

1. The user enters an email address.
2. The `/email-verify` endpoint checks whether the domain is on the whitelist.
3. If allowed, an OTP is sent and a token is generated.
4. The user submits the OTP, token, password, and email to `/create-account`.
5. If everything matches, the account is created.

At first glance, everything looked secure.

The whitelist validation worked correctly. Non-approved email domains could not receive registration OTPs and therefore should not have been able to create accounts.

However, while exploring the application, I found another workflow.

The application contained an `/unsubscribe` endpoint where users could enter an email address to unsubscribe from communications. This endpoint also sent an OTP and returned a token in the response.

Unlike the registration flow, this endpoint accepted any email domain.

The interesting observation was that both workflows appeared to rely on the same OTP and token validation system.

Instead of using the registration process, I supplied a non-whitelisted email address to the unsubscribe functionality, received the OTP and token, and then used those values in the `/create-account` request.

The account was successfully created.

The email domain restriction had been completely bypassed.

The most interesting part is that no individual component was broken:

* The whitelist check worked correctly.
* OTP verification worked correctly.
* Token validation worked correctly.

The vulnerability existed because the registration workflow trusted tokens generated by an entirely different workflow.

As the platform offered a 14-day trial for every new account, an attacker could continuously create accounts using email domains that should never have been allowed, effectively obtaining unlimited trial access.

## What Makes It a Business Logic Bug?

Many vulnerabilities can be discovered by testing a single request.

Business logic bugs are often discovered by understanding multiple workflows and how they interact.

In the example above, there was no vulnerable parameter, no injection point, and no missing authorization check.

The problem was that the application assumed a token generated in one workflow would only ever be used within that workflow.

That assumption turned out to be false.

Business logic vulnerabilities are usually violations of assumptions rather than violations of code.

---

## The Paradox of Business Logic Bugs

One thing I have learned is that business logic bugs behave differently from most other vulnerabilities.

The more you try to force them, the more they seem to disappear.

* There is no universal checklist.

* There is no payload that guarantees success.

* There is no scanner that can reliably identify every business logic issue.

* When searching for SQL Injection, you test for SQL Injection.

* When searching for XSS, you test for XSS.

* When searching for business logic bugs, you often stop searching for vulnerabilities altogether and start searching for assumptions.

The best findings usually come from asking simple questions:

* Why does this workflow exist?
* What assumptions does it make?
* Can another workflow produce the same result?
* What happens if I perform actions out of order?
* What happens if I reuse data generated elsewhere?

---

## How I Approach Business Logic Testing

My approach is intentionally simple.

First, I try to understand what the application is trying to achieve rather than how it is implemented.

Then I map important workflows such as:

* Registration
* Authentication
* Password reset
* Billing
* Subscriptions
* Referrals
* Coupons
* Account management

After understanding the normal path, I begin looking for assumptions.

Can a step be skipped?

Can a token be reused?

Can data generated in one workflow be accepted by another?

Can actions be performed in a different order?

Can a process be repeated indefinitely?

Can a user reach the same outcome through an unintended path?

These questions have led me to far more findings than any payload list ever has.

---

## Final Thoughts

Business logic bugs are difficult to find because they are rarely visible from individual requests.

They emerge from relationships between workflows, assumptions, and unexpected user behavior.

In my opinion, the most effective way to discover them is not to think like a vulnerability scanner.

Think like a curious user.

Understand how the business works.

Understand why each feature exists.

Then start asking a simple question:

"What happens if I use this feature in a way nobody expected?"

That question has led to some of my best findings, and it continues to be the most valuable business logic testing technique I know.
